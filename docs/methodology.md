# Methodology

This doc explains the rationale behind the skill's design choices. It's written for someone deciding whether to adopt the skill for their own refractory illness research, not as user-facing instructions. The README and CLAUDE.md describe what the skill does. This describes why each piece is shaped the way it is.

The doc itself is a worked example of the discipline it advocates. If you find AI-coded vocabulary, em-dash asides used as stylistic flourishes, or filler openers below, that's a defect in the doc and worth flagging.

## Why "research partner" rather than "assistant" framing

The default framing for an LLM is assistant. You ask, it answers. You request, it produces. The relationship is asymmetric in favor of the user setting the agenda, and the model's job is to satisfy that agenda efficiently. For routine work this is fine. For refractory illness research it's actively bad. The user already has an agenda, and that agenda is mostly composed of the questions the user already knew to ask. Refractory cases are by definition the cases where the obvious questions have been asked and the obvious answers haven't worked. What's left is the territory the user can't fully map alone.

The skill's framing is postdoc-to-PI. The user is the principal investigator on their own case. They know things no one else knows: the texture of attacks, what the previous trials actually felt like at week three, which side effects were tolerable and which weren't, what the chart says versus what's true. The model brings something different. It has breadth of literature recall, and a tolerance for rigor and argument that the user can't bring to their own case. The relationship works when the model can push back, propose hypotheses the user didn't consider, and stress-test the user's working theories. None of that fits inside "I help you do what you ask."

The practical consequence is that a research partner sometimes refuses the immediate request in favor of the prior question. If the user asks for a list of treatment candidates before the standard ladder for the condition has been mapped, a partner says no, we need to do that first, here's why. An assistant produces the list. The list will be plausible and most of it will be useless. The frontier-scan gate, the why-not-standard-ladder rule, the evidence tags, the round-trip protocol are all expressions of this same shift. They're the postdoc's discipline, applied to a model that would otherwise default to compliance.

Fielding questions and forming hypotheses are different jobs. The skill is built for the second one. If what you want is the first, you don't need this skill, you need a faster web search.

## Why evidence tags plus applicability annotation

Evidence tags answer "how strong is the evidence?" An RCT outranks an observational study, a meta-analysis of RCTs outranks a single trial, a case series outranks a narrative review for grading purposes, and so on. Tagging is standard in evidence-based medicine and the skill encodes the standard hierarchy. What the skill adds is the second axis: applicability. A tag tells you the quality of the evidence for the population studied. Applicability tells you how much that population resembles the case at hand.

The two axes are orthogonal and both matter. A high-quality RCT can be low-applicability if the trial population was screened to exclude the case at hand. This isn't a hypothetical. Refractory patients, by definition, have failed the standard interventions. Most pivotal trials for chronic-illness preventives explicitly exclude patients with prior multi-class failure, because including them would dilute the effect size and risk the trial's primary endpoint. The mechanism is not bad science, it's regulatory pragmatism. The downstream consequence is that the patients with the most need have the least directly applicable evidence. If you grade only on the tag axis, every refractory question collapses to "follow the guidelines." If you also grade on applicability, the guidelines often score moderate-to-low for the case at hand, and other classes of evidence become visible. Post-failure cohort data. Off-label protocols that never appear in pivotal-trial reviews. Mechanistic rationale anchored to a phenotype the patient actually fits.

`[SPEC]` is its own tag rather than a sub-category of `[REV]` because reviews and speculation fail in different ways. A bad review is wrong about facts that exist in the literature; a `[SPEC]` claim is making a structural inference that isn't anchored in any specific paper. Conflating them lets a speculative claim borrow credibility from a citation that doesn't actually support it. Keeping them separate forces the writer to declare which kind of error the reader should worry about. `[UNVERIFIED]` is a third failure mode entirely, a citation that didn't round-trip, and it should never appear in clinician-facing output.

## Why PMID/DOI round-trip and the quote bank

The hallucination problem in medical AI is not abstract. A citation is an unforgeable claim that the cited paper exists and says X. Without a round-trip, the model can produce citations that look correct on every visible axis (plausible journal, plausible author names, plausible year, plausible PMID format) and are nonetheless fabricated. The fabricated citation is more dangerous than no citation at all, because the reader's epistemic guard goes down on seeing a PMID. In a clinical context, acting on a phantom citation is how you arrive at a treatment decision built on nothing.

The round-trip is the minimum bar. Fetch the abstract from a primary source (NCBI E-utilities for PubMed, the publisher for DOI), confirm the title and authors match, save the abstract to a paper note. This catches the most common hallucination mode, which is the plausible-but-nonexistent paper. It's slow. It's slow in the sense that adding fifteen seconds per citation across a research session is real friction. The skill imposes the slow path because the cost asymmetry is enormous: a few minutes of friction per session, versus a clinical decision built on a phantom paper.

Round-trip alone is not enough. The more subtle failure mode is claim drift. The paper exists, the citation is real, but the claim being attributed to the paper is stronger or narrower than the paper actually makes. The paper says efficacy was suggested in a small open-label series; the citing text says it was demonstrated in a randomized trial. The paper says response was defined as a 30% reduction; the citing text says 50%. The paper says the cohort was post-failure; the citing text drops the qualifier. None of these are caught by round-trip, because the paper does exist. They're caught by the quote bank. Each paper note carries verbatim sentences from the abstract or full text, and any claim stronger than `[SPEC]` has to anchor to a specific quote. If you can't find a quote that supports the claim as stated, the claim has drifted and needs to be weakened or dropped.

The quote bank is the discipline that catches the hallucination the round-trip doesn't. Together they're the floor. It's worth saying that even both together don't make a claim true. They make the claim auditable, which is a different and more achievable goal.

## Why the why-not-standard-ladder rule

By the time someone is doing this kind of research on themselves, they've cycled through the standard treatments for their condition. Often multiple times, often in combinations. The standard ladder is not a mystery. It's documented in guidelines, in review articles, and in the patient's own treatment history. What's missing is what comes after the ladder runs out.

When you ask an LLM for treatment candidates without a structural constraint, what you get back is mostly the ladder, paraphrased. The model has read the same guidelines you have, and absent a reason to do otherwise, it returns the high-prior items first. The candidates look plausible because they are plausible, in general; they're just useless for this case, because this case has already tried them. Without a rule that forces the model to specify how a candidate is different from a guideline-recommended option already cycled through, the candidate list collapses into noise.

The why-not-standard-ladder rule fixes this by requiring every candidate file to declare which of nine specific deltas it satisfies. The deltas cover the full space of "defensibly not just a repeat": never tried, inadequately tried (with the inadequacy specified), new formulation or route or dose strategy, specific combination logic, phenotype-specific rationale, post-failure cohort evidence, tertiary-center protocol, comorbidity-targeted, diagnostic probe. The list is closed by design. If a candidate doesn't fit one of these, it's almost certainly a ladder repeat under a different name, and the answer is to either reframe it (which will reveal the delta, if there is one) or drop it.

The rule has a second benefit beyond filtering noise. It makes candidate files reviewable by a clinician in a way that ladder-style suggestions are not. A clinician reading "drug X for condition Y" has to do all the work of figuring out why the patient is being asked to consider it. A clinician reading "drug X, post-failure cohort evidence, here's the cohort, here's why this case fits the cohort" has the argument in front of them. The rule shapes the candidate into something a clinician can engage with quickly, which is the whole point of the pre-visit pipeline.

## Why the mandatory frontier-scan first session

Two project-critical artifacts cannot be reliably generated from training data alone. The first is the set of operational definitions for the condition: what counts as response, what counts as an adequate trial, where the confound thresholds sit. The second is the standard treatment ladder. These are condition-specific, sometimes contested in the literature, and they update over time. A model can produce defaults that look right and are off in ways that matter. Response thresholds vary across conditions and across trial designs within a condition. Adequate-trial duration varies by drug class, sometimes by individual drug. The standard ladder evolves as new agents are approved and old ones move down the recommendation list.

If the project starts producing candidates before this baseline is set, every downstream artifact is built on shaky foundations. The why-not-standard-ladder rule can't be applied without knowing the ladder. Applicability annotations can't be calibrated without knowing what the patient population looks like operationally. Adequacy classifications in the treatment-history file can't be trusted either, because they depend on the same definitions. The frontier scan is a one-time cost, usually a session or two, that pays off across the rest of the project's life. Once it's done, every candidate file references the same standards and every applicability annotation uses the same population definition. The shared baseline is what lets the project accumulate signal instead of recomputing context every session.

The gate is structural rather than just prose-based for a specific reason. CLAUDE.md prose is read by the model, but model adherence to prose instructions varies across versions and varies across users (a user who skims past the gate language will not be reminded of it). A status file with a machine-readable `status: incomplete` field that the model is instructed to read before any candidate-creation work is harder to skip. It's not impossible to skip. A user can edit the file. But the act of editing the file is the act of declaring that the scan is done, which surfaces the choice in a way that prose-only enforcement does not.

The friction is one-time and the benefit is permanent. That ratio is what makes it worth the upfront cost.

## Why the outgoing-communications anti-AI-tells

There's a meta-irony in this section. The doc you're reading was generated by AI, and it's about how to make AI-generated text not read as AI-generated. The irony is intentional. The doc has to pass the same audit it advocates, or the audit isn't worth advocating. If you've gotten this far without flagging em-dash overuse or hedging stack-ups, that's evidence the discipline can be applied to its own description. If you have flagged something, the doc has a defect, and the defect proves the audit's value.

Clinicians have started recognizing AI-coded text. The patterns are visible at the sentence level: em-dashes used as stylistic asides, hedging stacked on hedging, vocabulary like *robust* or *seamless* in places where plain English would land harder. They're also visible at the document level: filler openers, meta-explanations of why the writer is writing, closing politeness padding that adds words and removes specificity. A clinician message that smells like ChatGPT lands worse than a plainly imperfect human one. Part of the reason is credibility, the reader noticing that they're being addressed by software. The other part is information loss; the AI-coded register tends to smooth over the specifics that make a clinical message useful.

The skill's rule is that the user writes outgoing messages, not the model. The model's role is bounded to drafting and audit. The final text is the user's. This matters because the substance of a clinician message (the specific symptom, the specific date, the specific number, the specific question) is what makes it actionable, and that substance is the first thing AI prose smooths away. The user knows the substance. The model can help with structure and audit. Keeping the model out of the final draft is what preserves the human voice that gets the message read and answered.

The anti-tell list is empirical. Every item on it is a pattern that's been observed to mark text as AI-generated. The list will need updating as models drift and conventions change. The point of writing it down explicitly is that it can be audited and updated, rather than living as tacit knowledge that decays as the user assumes the model has it covered. The model does not have it covered. The list and the audit step are how the user keeps the model honest about its own register.
